## Part 1: Introduction

As engineers progress through their careers, the nature of their responsibilities begins to change.

Junior engineers are often measured by their ability to complete tasks:

* building pipelines
* fixing defects
* implementing features
* and delivering assigned work

Senior engineers begin thinking at a systems level:

* architecture
* scalability
* maintainability
* operational reliability
* and long-term technical direction

But technical leadership introduces another dimension entirely.

At scale, successful engineering delivery depends on more than writing good code. It requires the ability to organize projects, align stakeholders, coordinate execution, manage risk, and guide systems from concept to production.

This is where technical project management becomes an engineering skill.

The most effective data engineers eventually become more than pipeline developers. They become translators between business and technology, organizers of complex delivery efforts, and stewards of operational systems.

Over the years, I’ve developed a lightweight project methodology for managing enterprise data engineering initiatives. The framework is intentionally simple and focuses on the major phases that guide a project from initial alignment through production operations.

The methodology is divided into five sections:

| Phase   | Focus                                                                            |
| ------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Define  | Ownership, stakeholders, goals, and high-level milestones                        |
| Design  | Business requirements, user stories, technical architecture, and data modeling   |
| Execute | Task breakdowns, implementation planning, and sprint organization                |
| Test    | Unit testing, data validation, reconciliation, and User Acceptance Testing (UAT) |
| Go-Live | Deployment, monitoring, operational support, and maintenance                     |

Each phase builds upon the previous one.

The Define phase establishes alignment and ownership before implementation begins.

The Design phase translates business objectives into technical requirements through user stories, architecture diagrams, data flows, and logical and physical data models.

Execution transforms those designs into organized implementation work through task management, sprint coordination, and milestone tracking.

Testing validates not only whether pipelines run successfully, but whether the resulting data can be trusted by the business.

Finally, Go-Live focuses on operational ownership — monitoring, support processes, deployment readiness, and long-term maintenance after production release.

This framework is not intended to replace Agile, Scrum, or formal project management methodologies. Instead, it provides a practical structure for technical leadership within enterprise data engineering projects.

Because ultimately:

> Junior engineers often focus on tasks. 
> Senior engineers focus on systems. 
> Technical leaders focus on delivery.
